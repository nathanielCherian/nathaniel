** Publish imperfect work, and be proud of it. **

It is fun to work on personal projects that are not driven by neccessity or obligation. We work on personal projects for our own sake. These projects are useful to build skills and in some fields (computer science), they are almost required for career growth. 

When people imagine a project, they think about some new application or large codebase that someone maintains for a long time, like an open-source project or a web-application/service. But, projects don't have to be grand, nor do they have to be perfected and polished. These preconcieved notions of what a project should be contribute to the hestiation of actually taking the first step of actualizing an idea.

There needs to be a class of short-lived personal projects with zero expectations.

This introduces the idea of sketches (inspired by ![Mr. Doob ](https://mrdoob.com/).). I've used sketches as a way to practice implementing my ideas without the long-term commitment of a project. Additionally, sketches allow me to focus on the technical "meat" of a project, often the part that is the trickiest to solve that the entire sketch revolves around, without having to worry about peripherial issues (like a user interface).


Projects are subject to the 80/20 rule. 80% of your development time is directed towards  20% of the work. 

I still enjoy working on larger long-term projects. Often, this is because I have an idea that I want others to use and enjoy. 



- This introduces the idea of sketches. I want to be able to try building something quickly, that exercises some skill or scratches an itch, or if I just want to see how I would approach it. This is similar to how an artist has a cheap disposable sketchbook to jot down random ideas without any pressure of effecting their value as an artist.

- Inspired by Mr. Doob's sketches ![s](https://mrdoob.com/).

- Most of my sketches have a short production timeline of 1-2 days. This is intentional. The lifecycle of a sketch usually begins when I wake up with an idea for something. I usually spend time during the day trying to build it. I usually jump into it quickly, with very little planning. Usually by the next morning or evening, the sketch is complte and published to my website.

Polishing work is an important skill that should not be lost, but the tedius burden of refining work should not be the blocker between your ideas and the page.
